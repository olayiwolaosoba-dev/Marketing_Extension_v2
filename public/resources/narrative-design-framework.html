00 · Orientation

Why most B2B narratives fail

After auditing the messaging of 30+ Fintech and B2B SaaS companies, the same patterns keep appearing. Understanding these failure modes is the first step to escaping them.

❌ What fails

"We are a leading platform for…"

Feature-first narratives position you against every competitor who does the same thing. If a prospect can swap your name for your competitor's, you have no story.

✓ What works

Define the problem the market doesn't know it has

The best narratives identify an enemy — an old way of doing things — and position your product as the only logical escape route. You're not selling software; you're selling a future.

❌ What fails

Messaging hierarchy starts at product level

"Our API processes X transactions per second." Great. So does your competitor's. Feature-level differentiation collapses under competitive pressure.

✓ What works

Lead with category, land with proof

Start with why the category you play in matters. Then why you're the best in it. Then use product features as proof of the promise — not as the opening move.

❌ What fails

Separate narratives for each audience

When founders, sales, PR, and marketing are telling four different stories, the market hears noise. Misaligned narratives bleed trust faster than anything else.

✓ What works

One master narrative, many adaptations

One story at the top. Everything else — pitch decks, press releases, paid ads, sales calls — are different expressions of the same master narrative.

01 · The Architecture

The 3-part story architecture

Every great B2B narrative — from Salesforce's "No Software" to Stripe's "payments infrastructure for the internet" — follows this three-part structure. It's not a coincidence.

⚔

The Enemy

The status quo. The old way. The thing that exists now that is costing your customer time, money, or growth. Give it a name.

→
🧭

The New Way

Your category. Your product. The paradigm shift you represent. This is your POV — what you uniquely believe the world needs.

→
✦

The Future

What the world looks like for your customer after they adopt your product. Paint a specific, credible, and aspirational picture.

Real Example
Enemy: "Compliance is treated as a checklist — expensive, slow, and disconnected from growth. It sits outside the business."
New Way: "Compliance as infrastructure. Built-in, not bolted-on."
Future: "A business where every product decision is compliance-aware by default, and regulatory risk is off the CEO's worry list."
Notice how none of this mentions a feature. It maps a journey — from a problem the customer lives with, through a paradigm shift, to a world they want to live in.
02 · Workshop Modules

The five workshop modules

Run these five modules in sequence with your leadership team. Bring your CEO, Head of Sales, and Head of Marketing. Block a half day. Don't rush it.

1

Define the Enemy

The status quo that must die
45 minutes

The enemy is not your competitor. The enemy is a behaviour, a belief, or an old way of doing things that your customer has accepted as normal — even though it's costing them. Find it.

Workshop Prompts
1. What is your customer doing today that they'll look back on and call embarrassing in 5 years?

2. What does your best customer tell you their biggest frustration was before they found you?

3. If your product didn't exist, what expensive, manual, or broken process would your customer be stuck with?

It costs customers:
Nobody talks about it because:
2

Name the Category

Create or claim your space
30 minutes

You are either creating a new category (a way of doing things that doesn't have a name yet) or repositioning within an existing one (redefining what the category should mean). Both are valid. Neither is accidental.

Creating a category is expensive — it requires educating the market. Repositioning is cheaper — there's already demand, you're just redirecting it. Know which one you're doing before you spend.

Workshop Prompts
1. If a journalist had to write a headline about what your company is creating or changing, what would it say?

2. When a customer recommends you to a peer, which category do they put you in? Is that the right one?

3. What is the one-word category name you want to own in 3 years?
Your Output
Our category:
Creating or entering:
We will own it by:
3

Build Your Point of View

What you uniquely believe
45 minutes

A Point of View (POV) is a belief about the world that your company holds — and that your customer should hold too. It's not a product pitch. It's a stake in the ground. A POV gives your brand a perspective that attracts aligned customers and repels misaligned ones.

The POV Formula
"We believe that [status quo belief or behaviour] is [broken / outdated / costly]. The companies that will win in [time frame] are those that [new way of doing things]. That's why we built [company/product]."
This is your manifesto. It should be provocative enough to make some people uncomfortable and inspiring enough to make the right people nod vigorously.
Draft Your POV
We believe that:
This is broken because:
The companies that win:
That's why we built:
4

Create Proof Architecture

Make the story credible
30 minutes

A great narrative without proof is marketing. A great narrative with proof is positioning. Proof architecture is the deliberate selection and sequencing of evidence that makes your story impossible to dismiss.

Proof comes in four layers: customer proof (case studies, testimonials), data proof (statistics, outcomes), expert proof (awards, press, partnerships), and product proof (features that are only possible because of your POV).

Your Proof Inventory
Customer Proof: Which 2–3 customer stories best prove your narrative? What's the specific, measurable outcome?

Data Proof: What numbers do you have that nobody else can cite? (Your own research, your platform data, your benchmark report.)

Expert Proof: Who would most credibly validate your POV? Which press outlet, analyst, or institution would make your prospect say "OK, they're for real"?
5

Cascade to Channels

One story, many expressions
30 minutes

Once the master narrative is locked, every channel expression becomes an adaptation — not a reinvention. The core story stays constant; the format, length, and emphasis shifts for the context.

Channel Narrative Expression Lead With
Homepage Category + Enemy + New Way in the hero section. Proof below the fold. The transformation promise
LinkedIn (Company) POV posts, industry takes, data-backed opinions. Not product updates. The controversial belief
Investor Pitch Market timing (why now), enemy (market failure), new way (your category), proof (traction), future (the upside). The market insight
Sales Deck Customer's world (enemy), discovery challenge (pain), new way (your product), proof (case studies), next step. The customer's problem
Press Release Category angle first ("The first company to…"), then product, then quote that reinforces POV. The industry shift
Paid Ads Hook on the pain (enemy), intrigue on the solution, CTA to proof (case study, demo). The pain point
Content / Blog POV-led articles that educate the category and prove you understand the problem more deeply than anyone else. The insight nobody else has
03 · Implementation

After the workshop: making it stick

A workshop without implementation is just a good meeting. Here's how to make the narrative a living thing inside your organisation.

Week 1–2
  • → Document master narrative in a single "Story Bible" doc
  • → Share with CEO, Sales, Product — get sign-off
  • → Audit current homepage against new narrative
  • → Update LinkedIn bio, About page, and boilerplate
Month 1–3
  • → Rewrite homepage hero and sales deck
  • → Publish a POV-led article or manifesto piece
  • → Train SDRs/AEs on new messaging — record a session
  • → Run message testing with 5–10 target prospects
Month 3–6
  • → Commission 1–2 POV-led case studies
  • → Brief PR agency or journalist with category angle
  • → Build a research report or data asset around your POV
  • → Review win/loss data: is the narrative resonating?
